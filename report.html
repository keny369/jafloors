What this is

This is the static HTML mockup of the new JA Floors website. It contains nine pages built to the structure and scope defined in our Option A agreement. The pages use placeholder images from placehold.co, which will be replaced with real project photography as you supply it. The layout, navigation, content hierarchy and copy direction are all set and ready for your review.

Once you have reviewed these pages and confirmed you are happy with the structure and direction, we will move into the full Ruby on Rails implementation. Nothing in the final build will change structurally from what you see here unless you ask for it. The purpose of this mockup stage is to lock the architecture before writing production code, not after.

The mockup is published as static HTML on GitHub Pages so you can click through the full site on any device and see exactly how it reads and flows.

Site architecture

The site has nine pages arranged around four structural ideas that came directly out of the research we did before writing the options paper.

First, sector-first navigation. The current jafloors.com.au site leads with service pages: concrete polishing, concrete grinding, floor levelling, epoxy flooring, commercial vinyl. That structure positions JA Floors as a broad trade service. The new structure leads with sectors: healthcare, kitchens, fit-outs, controlled environments. That shift means a builder or facility manager sees their own environment in the navigation before they see a list of products. Every competitor we reviewed that performs well commercially does some version of this.

Second, capability grouping instead of service listing. The old service pages (concrete grinding, floor levelling, epoxy, vinyl, grind and seal) have been consolidated into four capability buckets: substrate preparation, vinyl/rubber/wall sheet systems, resin and protective coatings, and concrete finishes. This follows the content architecture review recommendation to organise capability around buyer relevance rather than SEO service labels. The old page titles like "Concrete Grinding Across Melbourne" drag the positioning back toward commodity trade language. The new structure does not.

Third, a qualified enquiry path. The current site uses a generic contact form and "free quote" framing. The new site separates Contact (light, secondary) from Request a Quote (structured intake form with project type, sector, size, stage, timing). The competitor review identified weak lead capture as one of the biggest gaps across the Melbourne commercial flooring market. This form does not need to be complex, but it does need to ask the right questions so Josh can triage enquiries and respond with something useful.

Fourth, legal pages kept off the sales path. Privacy Policy and Terms and Conditions stay live but sit in the footer only. They do not appear in the main navigation. This is standard practice and the content review recommends it explicitly.

Page by page

Homepage

View page

The homepage has five sections, each with a specific job. The hero establishes who JA Floors is and what environments they work in. The line "Commercial flooring for fit-outs, healthcare and specialist environments" comes directly from the capability statement and does more explanatory work in one sentence than a shorter tagline would. A first-time visitor from a commercial background should be able to read that headline and know immediately whether this business is relevant to them.

Below the hero, a trust strip carries three short proof points: geographic scope (Melbourne), service scope (preparation through installation), and sector focus (healthcare, kitchen, fit-out and controlled environments). We removed "Established 2006" from an earlier version because the logo already carries "Est. 2008" and the short text was throwing off the visual balance of the strip.

The sector cards are the centre of the page. Four cards, one for each target sector, each linking to its own sector page. This is the mechanism by which the site stops reading as "we do everything" and starts reading as "we understand your environment." The competitor review and content architecture review both treat this as the single highest-priority structural change.

The capabilities summary gives a high-level view of the four capability buckets without repeating the detail from the Capabilities page. It is there so a visitor who does not click into a sector page still sees that JA Floors covers preparation, vinyl systems, resin systems and concrete finishes.

The page ends with a quote CTA. Every page on the site ends with a path to the quote form or a phone number. There is no dead end.

Healthcare and Aged Care

View page

The four sector pages all follow an identical structure. This is deliberate. It reduces design cost, reduces review cycles, and makes the site feel composed rather than improvised. The healthcare page is the template for all four.

Each sector page has three sections: environment challenges (what the floor has to handle), relevant systems (which products and methods suit this environment), and how we work (a short paragraph on how JA Floors delivers in this type of setting).

For healthcare specifically, the copy speaks to hygiene, cleanability, durability under wheeled traffic, slip resistance, compliance awareness, noise reduction, and minimal disruption in occupied facilities. It does not claim certifications or compliance credentials that JA Floors does not hold. The content review is explicit about this: the tone should be suitability-focused, not compliance-theatrical. The systems listed are the ones actually relevant to healthcare environments (sheet vinyl, safety vinyl, rubber, wall sheet, coved skirting).

Same structure as healthcare. The copy shifts to wash-down suitability, slip resistance in greasy conditions, chemical resistance, thermal shock, and fast turnaround. The content review says this page should feel operational, not decorative. The systems listed reflect that: polyurethane resin, epoxy, safety vinyl, coved skirting, drain integration.

Office Fit-outs and Refurbishment

Clean Rooms, Labs and Controlled Environments

View page

Same structure. The copy uses disciplined language around precision, surface consistency, seamless transitions, chemical resistance and installation sequencing within commissioning timelines. The content review says explicitly: do not overclaim specialist certifications. The current copy follows that instruction. JA Floors can talk about the quality of their preparation and finish work without pretending to own pharma-grade certifications.

Capabilities

View page

Four sections, one per capability bucket. Each section answers three questions: where it fits, why it matters operationally, and which environments it suits. This structure replaces the old service pages (concrete polishing, concrete grinding, grind and seal, floor levelling, commercial vinyl, epoxy flooring) which the content review recommends retiring from the primary site architecture. Those old pages read as tactical service labels and in some cases drift into residential language (homes, garages, decorative concrete). The new capability structure keeps the same technical scope but frames it for commercial buyers.

About JA Floors

View page

The About page carries the company story, the process sequence (review, specify, prepare, install, handover), and four short blocks on what clients can expect. This is where "coordinated installation and site delivery" lives. In an earlier version of the brief, coordination was sitting in the capabilities taxonomy as a fifth bucket. We moved it here because it is a delivery trait, not a product or system class. The About page is the right home for it.

The process steps come from the capability statement and give a buyer a visible, simple model of how jobs are run. The competitor review identified that most commercial flooring firms in Melbourne do not make their process visible on their websites. Showing it is a low-cost trust signal.

Request a Quote

View page

The form captures: project type, sector, location, approximate size, project stage, timing, name, company, phone, email, and an optional free-text field for substrate or site condition notes. This is the "basic qualification" level specified in the options paper for Option A. It is not the full intake logic that sits in Options B and C.

The form is laid out in two columns on desktop so fields sit at a sensible width. Phone numbers and email addresses do not need to stretch across 900 pixels. Select fields are sorted alphabetically. The form ends with a single submit button and a secondary note pointing to the phone number and email for people who prefer to call.

Lighter than the quote page by design. Phone, direct line, email, service area, hours, and a callout box directing serious project enquiries to the quote form. The content review says Contact should feel practical and secondary to the quote path. That is what this page does.

Phone number and email decisions

The site uses a Melbourne landline (03) 9046 0618 as the primary phone number across all pages. Josh's mobile appears only on the Contact page, labelled "Direct Line - Josh Anderson."

The reason is positioning. A mobile number as the only contact across a commercial website signals sole trader to the types of buyers JA Floors wants to attract. Fit-out builders working for Tier 2 contractors and healthcare procurement officers are used to dealing with businesses that have office numbers. A Melbourne 03 landline reads as "established Melbourne business" without requiring a physical office or a receptionist. It can forward directly to Josh's mobile. The setup takes about ten minutes with any VoIP provider and costs a few dollars a month.

We considered a 1300 number but decided against it. In consumer services a 1300 number signals scale. In the commercial trades and construction space it can signal the opposite. Builders do not want to call a 1300 number and wonder whether they will reach a person or a call queue. A direct Melbourne number is more appropriate for this market and this business size.

The email across the site is enquiries@jafloors.com.au instead of Josh's personal address. Same logic: it reads as a business, not a person. It can forward to Josh's inbox. His personal email does not appear anywhere on the public site.

Photography recommendations

Every placeholder image on the mockup site will need to be replaced with real project photography before the site can do its job properly. The content architecture review is direct about this: the site's commercial credibility depends on showing real work in real environments. Stock photography of polished concrete bedrooms (which is what the current site uses) actively undermines the commercial positioning.

What to shoot

The site needs roughly 27 to 36 images spread across the following slots:

Homepage (8-10 images): One hero image of a finished commercial environment, four sector card images (one per sector showing that environment type), two to three capability/process shots, and one team or founder image for the trust strip area.
Each sector page x4 (3-4 images each, 12-16 total): One lead image showing the environment type, one to two supporting images (install in progress, finished detail, or before/after), one image for the "how we work" section.
Capabilities (4 images): One image per capability bucket. Substrate prep action shot, vinyl installation detail, resin application, concrete finish.
About (2-3 images): Founder portrait or team on site, one operational/supervision image.
Contact (1 image): Simple operational image or map. Could be skipped entirely.
Quote (0 images): None needed.

How to shoot

The branding pack recommends real project photography showing context rather than abstract material close-ups. That means a treatment room corridor with sheet vinyl installed, not a swatch. A commercial kitchen floor in use, not a texture sample. The environment should be visible around the floor so the viewer understands the setting.

Process images are valuable. The content review notes that competitors tend to show only finished glamour shots. JA Floors can differentiate by also showing preparation discipline: grinding in progress, levelling compound application, laser levels on substrate, crew working on a live commercial site. These images signal competence in a way that a polished finished photo alone does not.

Before/during/after sequences are ideal for the sector pages. Even two images showing the same area at different stages tell a stronger story than a single beauty shot.

Photo format and consistency

All images should be supplied as high-resolution JPEGs at a minimum of 2000 pixels on the longest edge. This gives enough resolution for full-width hero images on desktop screens and allows cropping for different aspect ratios across the site.

To make the photography feel cohesive across the site rather than like a random collection of phone snapshots, all images should be post-processed with a consistent treatment before going live. This does not need to be elaborate. A single Lightroom preset or equivalent applied across all images is enough. The treatment should:

Correct white balance so colours are neutral and consistent across different site conditions and lighting
Slightly desaturate to avoid oversaturated oranges and blues that look cheap on screen
Lift shadows gently so dark areas have detail without looking flat
Apply a very light grain to unify images taken on different cameras or phones
Straighten all horizontal and vertical lines (critical for flooring images where edges and walls are visible)
Crop to consistent aspect ratios matching the placeholder dimensions in the mockup

If Josh is shooting on a phone (which is fine for most of these), shoot in the highest quality setting, avoid zoom, shoot in good natural light where possible, and keep the phone level. Vertical lines that lean inward because the phone was tilted look amateur in any context but especially in construction and flooring photography where straight lines are the entire point.

We can handle the post-processing and cropping on our end if the raw images are supplied at sufficient resolution. A shared Google Drive folder sorted by sector and tagged with project type would be the simplest way to manage this.

How this connects to JA Floors' goals

The options paper was clear about the problem: JA Floors has real commercial capability and a base of repeat clients, but the current website presents the business as a broad flooring operator. The stronger competitors in Melbourne are not doing better work. They are presenting their work in a way that makes commercial buyers feel confident shortlisting them.

This mockup addresses that gap at the Option A level. It is not a full repositioning. It does not include formal positioning research, a brand identity refresh, case studies, a proof pack, or sales collateral. Those belong to Options B and C. What it does deliver is a structurally different website that:

Leads with sectors instead of generic service pages, so commercial buyers see their own environment in the navigation
Groups capability around buyer relevance rather than old SEO labels
Provides a qualified enquiry form that captures enough project detail to respond usefully
Removes residential language, generic "free quote" framing, and broad trade positioning
Uses a phone number and email structure that reads as an established commercial operation
Maintains a consistent, composed visual identity using the branding pack palette and typography

The competitor analysis showed that Lockwood, Floorcon, Asset Flooring Group and Contract Floors all use some form of sector-first or proof-led structure. JA Floors does not need to copy them. But the site does need to meet the baseline expectation that a commercial buyer has when they are shortlisting flooring contractors. This mockup meets that baseline.

It is a lean commercial qualification site. It is not a mini-repositioning platform. That distinction matters because it keeps the build within the agreed scope and budget while still delivering the structural shift that the research said was necessary.

URL migration note

The current site has standalone pages for concrete polishing, concrete grinding, grind and seal, floor levelling, commercial vinyl, and epoxy flooring. Those URLs will break when the new site goes live. Even if their search traffic is modest, basic 301 redirects should be part of the launch checklist so existing URLs resolve to the closest relevant destination on the new site. This is not a grand SEO project. It is basic hygiene. We will prepare the redirect map as part of the Rails build.
